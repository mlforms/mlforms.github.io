Ultrasound Artifacts

Created At：2022-03-25 09:37 Updated At：2023-04-19 19:12

Acoustic enhancement: Refers to hyperechogenicity deep to structures that transmit sound exceptionally well.

Acoustic shadowing: is characterized by a signal void behind structures that strongly absorb/reflect ultrasonic waves.

Bowel gas scatter

Refraction: Occurs when sound travels across one medium into another and changes speed. The sound waves are refracted at the boundary.

Edge Artifact: Often noted distal to smooth, rounded cavities containing fluid with a speed of sound transmission that markedly differs relative to surrounding tissue. This creates a refractile edge-shadowing artifact, characterized by narrow, hypoechoic, shadow lines extending distal to the lateral edges of the fluid cavity

Comet-tail: When reverberations occur between two closely spaced reflectors. A comet-tail artifact is a type of reverb artifact characterized by a series of closely spaced, discrete echoes.

Ring-down Artifact: Associated with gas bubbles. Resonance results in the bubbles becoming stimulated into vibration by the energy of the ultrasound wave. These vibrations result in near-continuous sound wave transmission which creates the echogenic line or lines extending parallel and distal to the bubbles.

Speckle: Results from interference effects of scattered sound within tissues. This grainy appearance results from the constructive and destructive interference of echoes returning simultaneously.

Mirror-image: The primary ultrasound beam encounters the pleural-air interface and is reflected back towards the liver and back towards the pleural-air interface. The resultant ultrasound image displays a duplicate structure equidistant from, but on the opposite side of, the reflective surface.
This can be used to rule out any fluid in the chest, as it appears only when the chest has no fluid. This makes the absence of a mirror-image artifact very helpful in diagnosing hemothorax or pleural effusion.


Beam-width Artifact: Arises from the progressive widening of an ultrasound beam distal to its focal zone. Eventually, the beam width may exceed the footprint of the transducer. A highly reflective structure that is encountered by this widened sound beam may erroneously be displayed within the narrow central imaging plane

Side lobe Artifact: where side lobes reflect sound from a strong reflector that is outside of the central beam, and where the echoes are displayed as if they originated from within the central beam.

Speed Displacement Artifact: When the ultrasound beam encounters tissue with a slower propagation speed (e.g., fat), the returning signal will take longer which results in echoes appearing deeper in the image than they actually are.
