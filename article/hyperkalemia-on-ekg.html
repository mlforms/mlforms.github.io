Hyperkalemia on EKG

Created At：2022-12-13 16:54 Updated At：2023-04-07 14:42

ECG findings in Hyperkalemia

  • Peaked T-waves (even when inverted)
  • Widening of the QRS (often marked)
  • Prolonged PR-interval
  • Flattening and eventual loss of P-waves
  • Bizarre tachydysrhythmias, pseudo ventricular tachycardia
  • Bizarre bradydysrhythmia, advanced AV Blocks and sinus pauses
  • Axis changes (especially RAD)
  • Fascicular & Bundle Branch Blocks
  • Pseudo ACS with ST-segment changes (can mimic STEMI)
  • Pseudo Brugada syndrome pattern
  • Sine wave morphology

Hyperkalemia produces predictable changes in ECG morphology based on the serum potassium level. Referred to as the string sign, once the classic peaked T waves appear, as the potassium level increases, the PQRST complex is pulled apart as if attached to a string. The ECG will eventually start to slow, the QT interval will prolong and a sine wave will be noticeable shortly before cardiac arrest. First-line treatment in a patient with hyperkalemia and ECG findings of a wide complex rhythm is calcium. Calcium directly stabilizes the cardiac membrane by increasing the critical firing threshold required to produce an action potential in cardiac myocytes. Calcium stabilizes the cardiac membrane but does not decrease the amount of serum potassium. Calcium is available for injection as either calcium gluconate or calcium chloride.
A Cochrane review recommends calcium chloride as the preferred treatment agent in the setting of cardiac dysrhythmia due to a 3-fold difference in the calcium bioavailability between the gluconate and chloride solutions. The preferred route of administration for calcium chloride is via central access since extravasation into peripheral tissue can lead to necrosis. Calcium gluconate is safe to administer via peripheral access.
